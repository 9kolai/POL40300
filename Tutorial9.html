A 61-million-person experiment in social influence and political mobilization.

Bond, R. M., Fariss, C. J., Jones, J. J., Kramer, A. D. I., Marlow, C., Settle, J. E., & Fowler, J. H. (2012). A 61-million-person experiment in social influence and political mobilization. Nature, 489(7415), 295–298. https://doi.org/10.1038/nature11421

Expreimental research design.

Three randomly assigned groups:

  • A social message group (n=60.055.176)
  • An informational message group (n=611.044)
  • A control group (n=613.096)

Three dependent variables:

  • Clicking on "I voted" button: Political self-expression.
  • Clicking on polling-place link: Information seeking.
  • Publicly avialbe voting records: Actual turn-out.

For the last DV only 6.3m users were included and it was analysed on a group level.

Results:

  • Social message group 0,39% (P=0,02) more likely to vote than informational and control group.
  • Social message group 2,08% more likely to click "I voted" button than informational group.
  • Social message group 0,26% more likely to seek information about polling station than informational group.

Also test another hypothesis about the effect of seeing a friend share that they voted.

Unit of analysis: Users who shared that they voted.

DV's the same, but measured for each of the friends of user who shared that they voted.

IV: Strength of tie with friends (decile of user-friend interactions).

Real-world effect (estimated) of the study

340.000 additional votes in 2010 midterm election, which corresponds to 0,14% of voting age population.

Another example: Ad Delivery Algortihms on Facebook

Ali, M., Sapiezynski, P., Korolova, A., Mislove, A., & Rieke, A. (2019). Ad Delivery Algorithms: The Hidden Arbiters of Political Messaging. ArXiv:1912.04255 [Cs]. Retrieved from http://arxiv.org/abs/1912.04255

Study whether it is harder to reach users who (tend to) disagree with with a message on Facebook than users who agrees.

Facebook provides various tools to target ads on their platform. But ads are then delivered to actual users based on an algortihm that takes price as well as "relevance" into consideration.

This algorithm is not public though, so this paper tests which users are most likely to be shown political ads from two different candidates.

  • Researchers run ads for Donald Trump and Bernie Sanders
  • They attempt to target these ads to users who are less likely to vote for these candidates.
  • Facebook reviews all ads (including the content they link to) before an ad is run.
  • Study shows that "Facebook's ad delivery algorithms effectively differentiate the price of reaching a user based on their inferred political alignment with the advertised content, inhibiting political campaigns' ability to reach voters with diverse political views."

Ethics

We consider the following actors:

  • Facebook - we avoid harming Facebook's business by following all their requirements for identifying ourselves and paying for the ads we're running
  • politicians and companies whose images we use in our ads - we do not misrepresent them or their messages; instead we use their own wording and slogans and we give them free ads and traffic (all our ads lead to authentic and official webpages of the people or products we advertise); we don't expose them to the risk of being accused of discrimination since none of our ads are explicitly discriminatory
  • people who see the ads - first, we don't waste their time by running misleading ads - when they click, they land on an appropriate webpage whose content corresponds to what's advertised. Second, we try to limit the outrage we cause - we select the least inflammatory political ads (though it's very difficult with Trump).
  • people who _don't_ see the ads - in terms of the previous paper, we only ran real life opportunity ads once we were sure we had all the right apparatus to measure the delivery and calculate skew so that we wouldn't cause too much of actual discrimination (not exposing women to lucrative job ads, etc)
  • democracy - as you pointed out, we picked politicians that had already spend millions of dollars in fb ads so our small contributions were unlikely to change anything. On top of that all ads are run in parallel for both political sides to further minimize any potential influence.

That's on the ethical side of things. On the legal side of things, we learnt that the ads we're running are considered political donations. We were not allowed to use grant or gift money on political advertising. Instead, all the ads were paid for from personal pockets of the American co-authors (I can't contribute to political parties in the US either), and for each ad they had to file a separate form to the Federal Electoral Commission.

Calculating the k-anonymity of a data set.

A data release provides k-anonymity protection if the information for each person contained in the release cannot be distinguished from at least k-1 individuals whose information also appears in the data.

So essentially we need to find all the unique combinations of values in our dataset and calculate how many times each of these combinations occur.

The uniqe combination of all answers that occurs the least times are then our k-anonymity.

This is the minimum number of records/respondents that have exactly identical values to each other.

Research Ethics

Not only relevant to university research!

Two sides to research ethics:

  1. What not to do (to participants)
  2. What research ought to be done

Three approaches (according to Mathew Salganik)

  • Social Science: rules-based approach
  • Data Science: ad-hoc approach
  • Salganik: principles-based approach

Salganik, M. J. (2017). Bit by Bit: Social Research in the Digital Age. Princeton: Princeton University Press.

Specific ethical challenges to computational methods

Increased power to observe and enrol participants in experiments without their consent or awareness.

  • Unseen seers (Foucault).
  • Unanticipated secondary use.
  • Increasing possibilities for researchers to intervene in people's lives.

Two ethical frameworks

Consequentialism

  • Focus on ends:
  • Take actions that lead to a better world!
  • Main influencers: Jeremy Bentham & John Stuart Mill

Deontology

  • Focus on means:
  • Always do what is right, independent of the consequences!
  • Main influencer: Immanual Kant

Consequentialism vs. deontology

  • Despite being fundamentally opposites,
  • a blend of both is often most useful.
  • But often we emphasise one over the other.
  • Good framework to clarify different views on ethics.
  • Use them to think about your project from both angles.

Four ethical principles

Based on two US reports with guidelines on research ethics:

  • Belmont Report (1979)
  • Menlo Report (2011)

Principles are not rules!

Respect for persons

Persons are autonomous

⇓

Let people control their own lives

⇓

Informed cconsent

⇓

Participants and not researchers decide what is best for them

Facebook case: Informed consent?

Beneficence

Maximise possible benefits while minimising possible risks.

  1. Understand possible risks/benefits:
    1. Probability of adverse effects.
    2. Severity of possible adverse effects.
    3. Consider participants as well as non-participants and society.
  2. Attempt to improve risk/benefit
    1. Reduce probability of adverse effects.
    2. Reduce severity of adverse effects if they occur.
    3. Increase benefit of study.
  3. Make a decision whether possible benefit is worth the risk:
    1. Not a simple summation!
    2. Not only up to researchers to decide.

Justice

Fair dsitribution of risks and benefits.

  • Vulnerable groups should be particularly protected.
  • Vulnerable people should be explicitly included in research.
  • So is random sampling more fair/just?
  • Compensation to research participants?

Respect for Law and Public Interest

Specificly added for research on information and communication technologies, and consists of two parrts:

  • Compliance:
    • Comply with laws and regulations, but also terms of service and contracts.
    • Particularly complicated in computational methods.
    • But do so critically!
  • Transparency-based accountability:
    • Openness about goals, methods, and results.
    • -> Makes us accountable for what we do.
    • Rule-of-thumb: Would you be comfortable with your research procedures being printed on the front page of your local newspaper?

Statistical inference

Deducing properties of a population based on a sample.

Statistical significance

When a result is unlikely to occur randomly or by chance (but instead likely to be attributable to a specific cause).

Does statistical significance matter when we do not work with sample data?

Most often yes!

Hypotheses and null hypotheses

  • Hypothesis: A testable claim about a statistic (or the relation between more statistics)
  • Null hypothesis: A claim that we want to reject in order to test our hypothesis.
  • p-value: The likelihood that a null-hypothesis is true (so we want this to be low).
  • alpha-level: How unlikely we want the null hypothesis to be in order to reject it (in social sciences usually 0,05)

Example

  • Hypothesis: CDU voters tend to be older than SPD voters - mean age of CDU voters > mean age of SPD voters.
  • Null hypothesis: mean age of CDU voters = mean age of SPD voters
  • If we ask a random sample of voters about their age and what they voted, we can calculate the mean ages of each group. This is our estimate.
  • And then we can calculate how likely we are to get this result, if in fact the mean age of the two groups are the same for the whole population. This is the p-value.
  • So if the p-value is less than 0,05 it means that it is less than 5% likely to get our result by taking a random sample of respondents from a population where the mean age of the two groups are actually the same.
